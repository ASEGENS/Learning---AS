Grammar

Particles are small words that indicate relations of words within a sentence. They follow other words such as nouns, verbs, adjectives to indicate an action, direction, purpose, or a point of time.

Particle Function Example
は (wa) Serves as the topic marker. It marks what you are talking about. 私は学生です。 (Watashi wa gakusei desu.) - I am a student.
が (ga) Serves as the subject marker. It marks the subject in a sentence. 私がリンゴを食べました。 (Watashi ga ringo wo tabemashita.) - I ate an apple.
を (wo) Direct object marker. It comes after the object that is being acted upon. 本を読みます。 (Hon wo yomimasu.) - I will read a book.
に (ni) Indicates a direction or target of an action, or a point in time. 学校に行きます。 (Gakkou ni ikimasu.) - I will go to school.
へ (e) Indicates a direction or goal of an action. 東京へ行きます。 (Tokyo e ikimasu.) - I will go to Tokyo.
で (de) Indicates a place where an action takes place, or a means of an action. 公園で遊びます。 (Kouen de asobimasu.) - I will play in the park.
と (to) Means "and" or "with". It is used to connect nouns or to indicate a companion for an action. 彼と映画を見ました。 (Kare to eiga wo mimashita.) - I watched a movie with him.
も (mo) Means "also" or "too". 私も行きます。 (Watashi mo ikimasu.) - I will go too.
から (kara) Means "from". It can also indicate a reason or cause. 学校から帰りました。 (Gakkou kara kaerimashita.) - I returned from school.
まで (made) Means "until" or "up to". It indicates a limit in space or time. 夜まで働きました。 (Yoru made hatarakimashita.) - I worked until night.
よ (yo) Emphasizes the certainty or importance of information. Used at the end of sentences. 明日、来ますよ。 (Ashita, kimasu yo.) - I will come tomorrow, for sure.
ね (ne) Seeks agreement or confirmation from the listener. Used at the end of sentences. いい天気ですね。 (Ii tenki desu ne.) - The weather is nice, isn't it?

Remember, the use of particles can change depending on the context of the sentence, and some particles can have other uses not listed here. Practice is key to mastering their usage.

In Japanese, counters or counting words are used along with numbers to count things, actions, and events. The counter word changes based on the type of object or concept being counted.

Counter Usage Example
本 (hon) Used to count long, cylindrical objects. 一本の鉛筆 (Ippon no enpitsu) - One pencil.
人 (nin) Used to count people. 三人の学生 (Sannin no gakusei) - Three students.
枚 (mai) Used to count flat, thin objects like paper or plates. 五枚の写真 (Gomai no shashin) - Five photos.
冊 (satsu) Used to count books. 二冊の本 (Nisatsu no hon) - Two books.
匹 (hiki) Used to count small animals. 四匹の犬 (Yonhiki no inu) - Four dogs.
台 (dai) Used to count machines or vehicles. 六台のコンピュータ (Rokudai no konpyuta) - Six computers.
杯 (hai) Used to count cupfuls or glassfuls. 七杯の水 (Nanahai no mizu) - Seven glasses of water.
歳 (sai) Used to count years of age. 二十歳 (Hatachi) - Twenty years old.

Remember, these are just some of the most commonly used counters. There are many more counters in Japanese, and some objects can be counted with different counters based on their specific characteristics or the context.

In English grammar, verbs can be classified into two broad categories: transitive and intransitive verbs. The distinction between these two lies in whether a verb requires an object to complete its meaning or not.

Transitive Verbs

Transitive verbs require one or more objects to complete their meaning. When a transitive verb is used in a sentence, it is often followed by a direct or indirect object.

Examples:

  • She reads a book. (The verb "reads" requires the object "a book" to complete the meaning.)
  • I bought a new car. (The verb "bought" requires the object "a new car" to complete the meaning.)

Intransitive Verbs

Intransitive verbs, on the other hand, do not require an object to complete their meaning. The action stops with the subject, and does not pass over to an object.

Examples:

  • The sun rises. (The verb "rises" doesn't require an object to complete the meaning.)
  • The baby slept. (The verb "slept" doesn't require an object to complete the meaning.)

It's worth noting that some verbs can be both transitive and intransitive, depending on their usage within the sentence.

One of the unique features of the Japanese language is its system of honorifics and the concept of keigo (honorific language). It reflects Japanese culture's deep-rooted sense of respect, politeness, and hierarchy.

Honorifics

Honorifics are a class of suffixes attached to names and pronouns to signify the level of respect towards the person being mentioned. Examples include "san", "sama", "kun", and "chan".

  • "San" - a general honorific, similar to "Mr.", "Mrs.", "Miss" in English
  • "Sama" - a more respectful version of "san", used for customers, deities, or when addressing someone in a much higher position
  • "Kun" - generally used for younger males, close friends, or subordinates
  • "Chan" - a diminutive suffix, used for children, close friends, or younger women

Keigo (Honorific Language)

Keigo is a style of speech used to show respect. It's more than just vocabulary or grammar; it involves a shift in the structure of sentences and the use of specific honorific and humble expressions.

There are three levels of keigo:

  • Polite language (teineigo): the basic level of politeness, used in daily conversation
  • Respectful language (sonkeigo): elevates the status of the person being referred to, used when speaking to superiors or customers
  • Humble language (kenjougo): lowers the status of the speaker to show respect to the person being spoken to, used when referring to oneself in conversation with superiors or customers

Keigo can be complicated and hard to master due to the multitude of rules and the need to adjust the level of respect based on the social context. However, its proper use is critical in Japanese society, as it reflects one's manners and understanding of the social hierarchy.

The Dictionary Form, also known as the Basic Form or Plain Form, is the form of the verb that you will find when you look up a verb in a dictionary. It is essentially the base form of a verb, and all other verb conjugations are formed using this base. In Japanese, verbs are categorized into three groups (Group 1, Group 2, and Group 3), and each group has a distinct pattern for how to form the Dictionary Form.

Group Verb Example Dictionary Form
Group 1 "待つ (matsu)" "待つ (matsu)"
Group 2 "見る (miru)" "見る (miru)"
Group 3 "する (suru)" "する (suru)"

In Dictionary Form, verbs from Group 1 usually end in 'u' vowel sounds (う, つ, る, む, ぬ, く, ぐ, す) like "待つ", verbs from Group 2 usually end with "る" like "見る", and verbs from Group 3 are irregular verbs, but two common examples are "する" and "来る (kuru)".

The Dictionary Form is typically used in casual conversation and can be used to express the present or future tense. For example, "明日、映画を見る" (Ashita, eiga o miru) translates to "I will watch a movie tomorrow". It's also used to describe habitual actions or general facts.

Remember that there are some exceptions and the context can change the way the Dictionary Form is used or understood. But don't worry, the more you use it, the more comfortable you'll get with identifying and using the Dictionary Form of Japanese verbs.

The "Masu" Form is an essential component of the Japanese language, used to express politeness in conversations. It is commonly utilized in formal settings, such as when speaking with superiors, in business situations, or when talking to someone you're not close with.

To form the "Masu" Form, verbs are modified according to their group classification. The process differs slightly for each group:

Group Dictionary Form Masu Form
Group 1 "待つ (matsu)" "待ちます (machimasu)"
Group 2 "見る (miru)" "見ます (mimasu)"
Group 3 "する (suru)" "します (shimasu)"

Group 1 verbs, or 'u' verbs, change the 'u' ending to an 'i' ending and add 'ます'. For example, "書く (kaku)" becomes "書きます (kakimasu)".

Group 2 verbs, or 'ru' verbs, simply replace 'る' with 'ます'. For instance, "食べる (taberu)" becomes "食べます (tabemasu)".

Group 3 verbs are irregular, but two common examples are "する" and "来る (kuru)", which become "します" and "きます (kimasu)", respectively.

The "Masu" Form is primarily used to indicate non-past affirmative action. However, it can also be used to express the negative, past, and negative past with appropriate conjugation.

Just like with the Dictionary Form, there are some exceptions to these rules. But once you have a good grasp of the "Masu" Form, you will have a solid foundation for engaging in polite and respectful conversation in Japanese.
